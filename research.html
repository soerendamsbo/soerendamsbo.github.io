Research

1. Mass media influence on the rapid rise of climate change

Published in International Journal of Public Opinion Research (2022) [link to article]

Abstract: When a political issue rises to the top of the public agenda, it puts pressure on decisionmakers, shifts power to actors engaging with the issue, and opens a window of opportunity. This article shows that online mass media contributed to the rapid rise of climate change on European public agendas in and around 2019. It also suggests that issue publics, i.e., particularly concerned and interested citizens, were influenced most. Conversely, citizens without strong climate opinions are not subject to influence from intensive media coverage. The results come from vector autoregression (VAR) models that take advantage of unique daily salience time series. The time series are constructed from weekly repeated nationally representative surveys of the public (N=25,445) and online news articles (N=6,905) by a dominant mass media news outlet that boasts a particularly large, trustful, and diverse audience. Traditional mass media can retain a powerful agenda-setting role even in increasingly fragmented media environments. But the target of their influence depends on the issue and the composition of corresponding issue publics, plausibly due in part to individual differences in use and processing of information about the issue.

2. How weather experiences strengthen climate opinions in Europe

Published in West European Politics (2021) [link to article]

Abstract: Previous research has shown that we believe more in the reality of climate change when we experience warmer-than-usual temperatures. This reflects a psychological process in which easily accessible information from personal weather experiences is used as a heuristic to form climate opinions. This paper replicates and extends upon a research design and results of Egan and Mullin to provide the first systematic European study of the Local Warming Effect. Based on data from 12 European countries, the analysis shows that when objective temperatures increase by two standard deviations (5°C) relative to normal temperatures, climate opinions are strengthened by around 0.5–1.0 percentage points – comparable to the effect of a full step to the left on a 0–10 political ideology scale.

3. When the election rains out and how bad weather excludes marginal voters from turning out with Kasper M. Hansen

Under review

Abstract: Ostensibly random and trivial experiences of everyday life, such as local weather, can have significant political consequences. First, a meta-analysis of 33 studies of rainfall and electoral turnout shows the average effect is a 0.76 %-points reduction in the probability of voting per centimeter of rainfall. Secondly, we analyze the first individual-level time series of validated turnout for a complete electorate merged with fine-grained meteorological observations. Bad weather reduces turnout by 0.84 %-points per centimeter of rainfall. Importantly, marginal voters – specifically young voters, except for first-time voters – are up to seven times more susceptible to bad weather influence and are also more responsive to pleasant weather (sunshine). This suggests that bad weather can exacerbate inequalities in democratic participation by pushing low-propensity voters to abstain. Thus, efforts to include marginal voters should be intensified at elections with (a high risk of) poor weather, and elections could even be moved to a time with more pleasant Election Day weather.

4. Voting for pro-climate parties and candidates in response to extreme flooding experience

Working paper (available upon request)

Abstract: Floods, heatwaves, droughts, and other extreme weather events are becoming more frequent, enduring, and intense due to human-caused global heating. While previous research has shown how first-hand experiences of such events influence citizens’ climate-related attitudes and perceptions, studies of their impact on actual behavior are few and reach mixed conclusions. This paper contributes with a twofold study that investigates the effects of an unusually destructive and salient storm surge event caused by a 2013 northern European winter storm on pro-climate voting in Danish elections. Employing a difference-in-differences design at the level of all 1,386 polling stations, Study 1 shows how the parliamentary election vote share of the pro-climate parties in the left-green bloc—which holds clear, persistent issue ownership on climate-related issues—increased by at least 1.5 percentage points in flooded communities. This effect furthermore increases with higher local flooding intensity. Study 2 employs a triple difference design at the level of all 9,554 local election candidates to show how flooding exposure produced a distinct electoral benefit for pro-climate candidates. Specifically, the storm surge caused the gap in election chances between candidates running on a pro-climate policy platform and non-climate candidates to increase by 5.5 percentage points in heavily flooded communities. Within the pro-climate parties, where climate change is a more salient concern, the gap is 11.2 percentage points. Together, the results paint a picture of significant behavioral change induced by extreme weather experience that shifts voters towards parties and candidates with pro-climate agendas.
